**Kitchen Prep is where you start.** The PIE Cookbook calls this the
*environmental scan*: before you cook, you look at what's already in the kitchen.
The single most common failure mode for first-time builders is reaching for a
tool before naming the problem — so this stage exists to slow that down.

<Aside type="note" title="From the source">
This adapts the *Kitchen Prep → Environmental scan* section of the
[PIE Cookbook](https://github.com/piepdx/pie-cookbook).
</Aside>

## Three questions, in order

<Steps>

1. **What do you already have?** Skills, relationships, existing tools, data,
   trust within the community. Most builders undercount this.

2. **What's the gap?** State the problem as a sentence a community member would
   recognize: *"organizers can't document evictions without exposing the
   tenant."* Not *"we need an app."*

3. **What's your working theory?** The smallest intervention you believe would
   move the problem. This is a hypothesis, not a spec — you'll revise it.

</Steps>

## Why this protects you later

If you skip Kitchen Prep, an AI agent will happily build *something* — but you'll
have no way to tell whether it's the right something, and no frame for saying no
to a dependency that doesn't fit. Naming the problem first is what lets the
[exclusion policy](/policies/) and the [recipes](/recipes/) do their job: they
can only protect a decision you've actually made.

## Don't categorize too early

The catalog's largest bucket is deliberately the vaguest — *Misc & Everything
Else* — because premature categorization hides options. During Kitchen Prep,
browse broadly; you're scanning the environment, not committing to a stack.

When your working theory is stable, move to [Cooking →](/pie/cooking/), where the
catalog's vetted ingredients are organized by what they're for.
