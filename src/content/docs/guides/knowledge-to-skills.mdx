You already hold the most valuable thing here: the wisdom in your community and
your field. The eviction-response checklist. The de-escalation steps. The way
your collective handles sensitive data. The tone your organization speaks in.
This page is about turning that — the stuff in your head and your binders — into
**agent skills** your AI agent follows every single time.

## What's an agent skill?

A **skill** is a short `.md` file that teaches your agent *one of your methods*.
Instead of the agent guessing how to do something, it reads your skill and does
it **your way** — consistently, for you and for everyone in your collective.

**A harness like Goose gives the agent hands. The catalog gives it vetted tools.
A skill gives it *your judgment.*** It's the difference between "an AI wrote
something" and "an AI applied our actual playbook."

<Aside type="tip" title="This is the whole ethos, made concrete">
We Can Just Build Things exists to augment your wisdom, not replace it. A skill
is exactly that: your experience, written once, reused forever — and it stays
yours.
</Aside>

## The tool: knowledge-to-skills-pipeline

[**knowledge-to-skills-pipeline**](/catalog/knowledge-to-skills-pipeline/) takes
your source material — books, guides, toolkits, manuals — and turns it into
structured `SKILL.md` files: a methodology your agent can apply, plus an
**attribution** block that credits where the knowledge came from.

It runs on **Anthropic Claude — a permitted provider** (never Meta, OpenAI, or
xAI), so making skills follows the same values as everything else here. Its own
reference skills are drawn from *Beautiful Trouble: A Toolbox for Revolution*.

## What a skill looks like

A `SKILL.md` is just plain text — YAML metadata at the top, then your method in
plain language:

```markdown
---
name: tenant-intake
description: Take an eviction report safely.
attribution:
  source: "Tenants Union field manual"
  license: CC-BY-SA-4.0
---

# Taking a tenant intake

1. Use a chosen handle, not a legal name.
2. Record the building, not the unit, at first.
3. Encrypt everything; two organizers hold keys.
4. Confirm what the tenant wants next.
```

The agent reads this and follows *your* intake process — not a generic one.

## Make your first skill

<Steps>

1. **Gather your source.** A manual, a field guide, an SOP, even good notes.
   The clearer your source, the better the skill.
2. **Run the pipeline.** Follow the [repo's
   instructions](https://github.com/MartinMontero/knowledge-to-skills-pipeline)
   to install and run it (it uses [Bun](https://bun.sh) and your own Anthropic
   key). It reads your material and drafts `SKILL.md` files.
3. **Review it — you're the expert.** Read the draft. Fix anything the model got
   subtly wrong. Your judgment is the point; the tool just does the typing.
4. **Keep the attribution honest.** If the method comes from someone's book or
   toolkit, credit it and respect its license (many movement resources are
   CC-BY-SA).

</Steps>

## Use your skills in a build

Every starter from the [Build Studio](/build/) already includes a `skills/`
folder with a template and a README. To put your own skills to work:

<Steps>

1. Drop your `SKILL.md` files into the project's `skills/` folder.
2. Point your agent at the project (Goose or Claude Code). The generated
   constitution and agent prompt already tell it to **read `skills/` first and
   follow your methods.**
3. Build. The agent now applies your playbook on every task — consistently,
   and accountable to the people who wrote it.

</Steps>

<Aside type="caution" title="Your method is law, not a suggestion">
Skills sit alongside the project constitution: the agent treats them as
instructions to follow, not ideas to improve on. If a skill conflicts with a
task, the agent should surface it and ask — never quietly do it its own way.
</Aside>
