You want a group chat where only the members can read the messages — no central
server, no phone numbers, and ideally not even a record of *who* is talking to
*whom*. That is exactly what **Marmot** is for: it runs the IETF **MLS** group-
encryption standard over **Nostr's** decentralized network. This guide explains
what the pieces are, how they fit, and — just as importantly — where the rough
edges are.

<Aside type="caution" title="Read this first: Marmot is experimental">
The Marmot spec says so in its own words: **"Marmot is currently experimental
software,"** it **"has not yet undergone formal security auditing,"** and its authors
**"recommend against using Marmot for production applications until the protocol
reaches stable status."** Treat what you build here as a prototype to learn from and
contribute to — not as a drop-in replacement for an audited messenger. (Pinned from
[`marmot-protocol/marmot`](/catalog/marmot/) README at commit `21a67b2`.)
</Aside>

## What Marmot is

**Marmot = MLS (the cryptography) + Nostr (the identity and transport).**

- **MLS** ([RFC 9420](https://www.rfc-editor.org/rfc/rfc9420.html)) is the IETF's
  Messaging Layer Security standard for end-to-end-encrypted *group* messaging. It
  gives you **forward secrecy** (compromising today's keys doesn't expose past
  messages) and **post-compromise security** (the group heals after a member
  rotates keys).
- **Nostr** provides the identity (your own keypair — no phone or email) and the
  transport (relays you choose, not a central server).

[Marmot](/catalog/marmot/) is the specification that ties them together — a set of
"MIPs" (Marmot's spec documents) defining which Nostr event kinds carry MLS
KeyPackages, group invites, and encrypted messages. [White Noise](/catalog/rust-lib-whitenoise/)
is the production reference client built on it.

## Why MLS over Nostr (and not something else)

Marmot's own framing: Signal has excellent E2EE but centralized infrastructure;
plain Nostr DMs (NIP-04/NIP-17) lack forward secrecy and real group messaging.
Marmot aims to combine **MLS's proven group cryptography** with **Nostr's
censorship-resistance and identity freedom** — encrypting both message *content*
and, as a goal, the *metadata* of who is in a group.

## The pieces, and how they fit

You do **not** implement any of the cryptography yourself. You assemble vetted,
license-verified libraries and wire up the Nostr event choreography. The crypto
lives in audited code; your job is integration.

| Layer | What it does | Use |
|---|---|---|
| **MLS core** | The actual group cryptography (RFC 9420) | [`openmls`](/catalog/openmls/) (Rust) · [`ts-mls`](/catalog/ts-mls/) (TS) |
| **Marmot engine** | Marmot's high-level API over MLS — KeyPackages, groups, Welcomes, message encryption | [`mdk-core`](/catalog/mdk-core/) (Rust, the engine inside White Noise) · [`@internet-privacy/marmot-ts`](/catalog/internet-privacy-marmot-ts/) (TS) |
| **Media** | Encrypted images/files, stored off-relay | [Blossom](/catalog/blossom/) protocol · a [Blossom server](/catalog/blossom-server/) · [`blossom-client-sdk`](/catalog/blossom-client-sdk/) |
| **Reference client** | The production app that proves it works | [White Noise](/catalog/rust-lib-whitenoise/) |

The moving parts, in plain terms (event kinds pinned from the MIPs at commit `21a67b2`):

1. **Identity → KeyPackage.** Your Nostr keypair becomes your MLS identity. You
   publish a **KeyPackage** (`kind:30443`) so others can invite you, and advertise
   *where* your KeyPackages live with a **KeyPackage relay list** (`kind:10051`).
2. **Group + Welcome.** A group is an MLS group tagged with the Marmot Group Data
   Extension (`0xF2EE`). To add someone, you fetch their KeyPackage and send a
   **Welcome** (`kind:444`) gift-wrapped for privacy (NIP-59).
3. **Messages.** Group messages (`kind:445`) are encrypted with a key the MLS
   library derives per epoch (ChaCha20-Poly1305). New members read them from the
   relays named in their Welcome.
4. **Media** (optional). Files are encrypted, stored on **Blossom** by their hash,
   and shared with a small `imeta` tag in the message.

The step-by-step choreography ships as four companion **skills** (and matching
**Goose recipes**) in this repo: `marmot-group-setup`, `marmot-relay-strategy`,
`marmot-encrypted-media`, and `marmot-push-notifications`. Each one orchestrates the
Nostr events and **delegates every cryptographic operation to MDK / marmot-ts** —
never reconstructing MLS by hand.

## Driving it from the Build Studio

Use the [Build Studio](/build/) to assemble the stack — pick your language (Rust
via MDK, or TypeScript via marmot-ts), add the catalog entries above, and export a
**Goose recipe**. Then run it in your own [Goose](/guides/get-started-with-goose/),
on your machine, with your own model and keys. The platform hands Goose a recipe; it
never sees your keys or your messages.

## Honest limitations

<Aside type="caution" title="Know these before you ship anything">

- **It's experimental and unaudited.** The protocol has had no formal security
  audit and its authors advise against production use (see the warning up top).
- **The spec is still moving.** MIP-00–03 (credentials, groups, welcomes, messages)
  are in **Review** and required; MIP-04 (media) and MIP-05 (push notifications) are
  **optional**, with push still a **Draft**. Numbers can change — re-pin from a
  named commit before you rely on them. (We already saw the KeyPackage kind move
  from a legacy `443` to today's `30443`.)
- **~150-member ceiling on Welcomes.** Per [MIP-02](/catalog/marmot/), *"for groups
  above ~150 participants, welcome messages will become larger than is permitted by
  many Nostr relays."* Large-group ("light Welcome") support is still being worked
  out upstream. Plan for small-to-medium groups today.
- **No first-class AT Protocol encryption in-repo.** Marmot's wire format is
  Nostr-specific (kinds `30443`/`444`/`445`). The MLS core is transport-neutral, so
  MLS *can* run over AT Protocol — but there is no Marmot AT-Protocol binding in the
  repos today.
- **White Noise and Germ do not interoperate.** White Noise speaks
  Marmot-over-Nostr; [Germ](/catalog/germ-lexicon/) is MLS-style messaging over AT
  Protocol. They share the *idea* of MLS but not the *wire* — they cannot exchange
  messages. Pick one stack per app; never assume cross-protocol interop.

</Aside>

## Choose your protocol by threat model

Both Nostr (Marmot) and AT Protocol (Germ-style) approaches inherit MLS's forward
secrecy and post-compromise security. The real decision is about **transport,
ecosystem, and maturity** — match it to *your* threat model, not to hype.

- **Your users' identities already live on Nostr; you value censorship-resistance
  and no phone/email.** → **Marmot / White Noise (Nostr).** You get decentralized
  relays you choose and Nostr-native identity. Accept that it's experimental and
  capped at ~150-member groups for now.
- **Your users live on AT Protocol / Bluesky and you want E2EE there.** → the
  **AT Protocol path** (e.g. Germ's lexicon). This is an even earlier, *separate*
  ecosystem — and again, not wire-compatible with White Noise.
- **You need audited, production-grade E2EE today, and decentralization is secondary.**
  → Be honest with yourself and your users: a formally-audited messenger is the
  safer choice right now. Come to Marmot to **build and contribute**, or when
  decentralization and Nostr identity outweigh maturity.

<Aside type="note" title="The rule">
Choose the protocol that matches the threat your users actually face — surveillance,
deplatforming, key compromise, metadata exposure — and tell them plainly which
guarantees hold and which are still experimental. Never imply two stacks
interoperate when they don't.
</Aside>
