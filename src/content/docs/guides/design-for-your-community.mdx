## Read this first

You are not a designer. Good. You have something better.

A professional designer parachuting into your project would know typography and
colour. But they wouldn't know *your people* — the neighbourhood, the
congregation, the co-op, the crew, the elders, the kids. You do. That knowledge
is the rarest and most important design input there is, and you already have it.

<CardGrid>
  <Card title="Your agent does the execution" icon="setting">
    It knows the technical rules — exact colours, spacing, sizes, accessibility
    code. You don't memorise any of it. (That's the [machine-facing
    spec](/policies/).)
  </Card>
  <Card title="You bring the judgment" icon="heart">
    You decide what matters, how it should feel, and whether it truly serves the
    people it's for. You're the director; the agent is the crew.
  </Card>
</CardGrid>

That's the whole skill: **seeing, deciding, asking.** This guide teaches all three.

<Aside type="tip" title="One promise">
Every time this guide names a design idea, it tells you (1) what it means in
plain words, (2) why it matters for *your* community, (3) how to spot it with
your own eyes, and (4) exactly what to say to your agent. No jargon you can't use.
</Aside>

## Move 0 — Start with your people, not the screen

Most design advice starts with buttons and colours. That's backwards. **Start
with who this is for**, because every later decision answers to them. Answer
these in a notebook in ten minutes:

- **Who exactly is this for?** Not "users." Real people: *"Tías in their 60s who
  run market stalls." "Teenagers organising a walkout." "Newcomers who speak more
  Tagalog than English."* The more specific, the better your design.
- **What's their real situation?** Old phone or new? Spotty connection on the bus?
  One shared family device? Bright sunlight? Reading glasses? A beautiful app that
  needs the newest iPhone is useless to a community on five-year-old Androids.
- **What do they actually need to *do* here?** Usually one or two things. Find them.
- **What would make them feel this was made *for them*, by their own?** That feeling
  is the target.

<Aside type="note" title="Why this is the most important move">
The big tech design systems are built for "everyone on Earth," which quietly
means *no one in particular*. Their defaults — blue links, clean sans-serif, stock
photos — are choices dressed up as neutral facts. They're not neutral. Building
for a specific community lets you make choices that fit *them* — and that's where
delight comes from. Generic is the opposite of loved.
</Aside>

**A practice, not a guess:** you don't have to imagine what your community wants —
ask them and show them. Your agent can generate three different looks in minutes.
Put them in front of real people. Let them point. **Designing *with* your community
beats designing *for* them, every time.**

## The craft: five moves that make things feel professional

You don't need talent for these. You need to know they exist and insist on them.
For each: **the idea → why it matters → how to spot it → what to ask your agent.**

### Move 1 — Make the most important thing obvious

**The idea.** On any screen, *one thing* should be most important; the eye lands
on it first. Everything else is quieter. (Designers call this *hierarchy*.)

**Why it matters.** When everything shouts, nothing is heard, and people freeze. A
clear screen feels calm and trustworthy.

**How to spot it.** Squint until the screen blurs. What still stands out? If the
*wrong* thing does — or five things tie — your hierarchy is off.

**Ask your agent:**
- *"Make [the main action] the clear focus; make everything else quieter."*
- *"When I squint I can't tell what's most important. Strengthen the hierarchy."*

### Move 2 — Less, but better

**The idea.** The fastest way to look amateur is to cram too much in; the fastest
way to look pro is to *remove* things and let what's left breathe. That breathing
room (*whitespace*) is doing the work.

**Why it matters.** Space makes content readable, calm, and premium. Think of a
cluttered dollar store vs. a calm boutique — same goods, opposite feeling.

**How to spot it.** Does it feel tight, busy, suffocating? Things touching the
edges and each other? Take something away and add room.

**Ask your agent:**
- *"This feels cluttered. Add more whitespace and breathing room."*
- *"What can we remove from this screen without losing the core purpose?"*

### Move 3 — Decide once, repeat everywhere

**The idea.** Pick your colours, spacing, button style, and text sizes *once*,
then reuse them everywhere. (The technical name is *design tokens*; think of it as
your "design kit.")

**Why it matters.** Consistency makes a product feel like *one* thing made by
people who care, and it builds trust — the same button always behaving the same
way. Inconsistency makes people uneasy without knowing why.

**How to spot it.** Click through a few screens. Do the buttons match? Is spacing
even? Four shades of almost-the-same blue? Mismatches are the tell.

**Ask your agent:**
- *"Set up a consistent design system — one set of colours, spacing, and button
  styles — and use it on every screen."*
- *"Use the same spacing rhythm everywhere so things line up cleanly."* *(Pros
  align to a steady 8-pixel rhythm — your agent knows how; just ask for consistency.)*

### Move 4 — Make it clear what to do, and what just happened

**The idea.** People should never wonder *"can I tap this?"* or *"did that work?"*
Interactive things should look interactive, and every action gets an immediate
response. (*Clarity and feedback*.)

**Why it matters.** Confusion is the #1 reason people give up. Clear cues and quick
feedback make a product feel like it's anticipating you, not testing you.

**How to spot it.** Hand your phone to someone who's never seen it and *say
nothing*. Where do they hesitate? What do they tap that isn't tappable? Every
hesitation is a problem you can now fix.

**Ask your agent:**
- *"Make it obvious which things are tappable and which aren't."*
- *"Add clear feedback for every action — a confirmation on success, a loading
  state while it works, a friendly message when something goes wrong."*

### Move 5 — Smooth the path through

**The idea.** A product is a *journey* from "I arrived" to "I did the thing I came
for." Map that path; make it short and obvious. Cut steps, guide forward, remove
dead ends. (*Flow*, or *UX*.)

**Why it matters.** Every extra step and every "wait, now what?" loses people. The
best products feel like a clear hallway with doors marked, not a maze.

**How to spot it.** Walk the main task yourself; count the steps and moments of
doubt. Fewer taps? Is the next step always obvious?

**Ask your agent:**
- *"Walk me through the main task. Where can we remove steps or make the next step
  clearer?"*
- *"Map the flow from landing to [the main goal]; make the path obvious and short."*

## Build it so everyone's invited

The industry calls this *accessibility*; a better word is **welcome**. It asks:
*who can actually use the thing you built?* That's not a technical question — it's
a values question. Every choice decides who gets to belong and who's quietly left
out.

Your community includes people with aging eyes, people who can't see at all,
people whose hands shake or who tap one-thumbed on a moving bus, people on cheap
screens in bright sun, people who get dizzy from motion. **They are not edge
cases. They are your people.**

The good news: your agent knows exactly how to do all of this. Your job is to
*insist on it* and hold the line.

- **Can people read it?** Strong enough contrast to read in sunlight, with tired
  eyes, on a bad screen.
- **Can people tap it without missing?** Targets big enough for real thumbs and
  shaky hands.
- **Can people who don't use a mouse get through it?** Full keyboard navigation,
  with a visible outline showing where they are.
- **Can blind people use it?** Screen readers work beautifully on a properly built
  page — but the agent has to do it on purpose.
- **Can people who get sick from motion turn it off?** Respect the device's
  "reduce motion" setting.
- **Can people read it in the dark, or in their language?** Dark mode; and if your
  community is multilingual, plan for it from the start.

<Aside type="caution" title="The one thing to remember">
When you skip accessibility you're not "saving time" — you're choosing who to
leave out. Make that choice on purpose, and make it inclusive.
</Aside>

**Your accessibility ask, every single build — paste this:**

> *"Make this meet WCAG 2.2 AA accessibility standards: readable contrast, large
> enough tap targets, full keyboard navigation with visible focus, screen-reader
> support, and respect for reduced-motion and dark-mode settings."*

The agent does the rest. You just have to mean it. *(Everything generated by the
[Build Studio](/build/) already carries this line into your agent's instructions —
so the floor is set before you write a word.)*

## Make it unmistakably theirs

This is where good becomes *stunning* — and the part no corporate design system
will give you, because their whole job is to look the same for a billion
strangers. Your job is the opposite: something that could only belong to *this*
community. This is *cultural relevance*, and it's not decoration — it's
recognition. The moment someone looks at the screen and feels *"oh — this was made
by us, for us."*

- **Colour carries meaning, and "tech blue" is a choice, not a law.** Communities
  already have palettes — in textiles, murals, market signs, landscapes, flags,
  the local football club. Borrow from what already feels like home.
- **Words should sound like the people, not a robot.** *"All set, see you Sunday!"*
  lands differently than *"Submission successful."* Write the way a trusted person
  *from* the community would say it — warm or formal, one language or fluidly
  switching, as they actually speak.
- **Show the actual people.** Real faces, ages, bodies, the neighbourhood — not
  stock models. Defaults erase people; specificity honours them.
- **Use metaphors that travel locally.** A "home" icon or "share" symbol doesn't
  read the same everywhere; sometimes a local image or word is far more intuitive.
- **Fit their real conditions.** Designing for old phones, shared devices, low
  bandwidth, and multiple languages *is* cultural relevance — respecting how people
  actually live.

<Aside type="caution" title="One serious caution">
Cultural relevance comes from *listening to your specific community*, not from
assumptions about "a culture." You cross the line into stereotype the moment you
stop asking real people and start guessing from outside. The safeguard is Move 0:
**build with them.** Show drafts. Ask. Believe them when they tell you it isn't
right.
</Aside>

**Ask your agent:**
- *"Move away from the generic tech look. Here's who this is for and what matters
  to them: [your community — their colours, voice, context]. Reflect that."*
- *"Rewrite all the text in a warm, [your community]'s voice — the way a trusted
  neighbour would say it."*
- *"Build a colour palette inspired by [a real source: a local mural, the
  landscape, traditional textiles], and check it still meets contrast standards."*
- *"Give me three visual directions so I can show them to people and pick together."*

## How to talk to your agent

You don't need design words. **Describe the feeling or the problem, then let the
agent translate.** The pattern that always works: *what you're seeing/feeling* +
*what you want instead*.

| What you feel | What to say |
| :--- | :--- |
| "It looks cluttered." | *"Add more whitespace; remove what's not essential."* |
| "I can't tell what's important." | *"Strengthen the hierarchy — make the main thing stand out."* |
| "It looks generic." | *"Move away from the default look; reflect [my community] — here's how…"* |
| "I don't know if I can tap that." | *"Make interactive elements clearly tappable."* |
| "Nothing happened when I tapped." | *"Add immediate feedback for that action."* |
| "It feels off but I can't say why." | *"Check consistency — are colours, spacing, and buttons the same throughout?"* |
| "It's too many steps." | *"Map this flow and cut steps; make the next step obvious."* |

Three habits that make you great at directing an agent:

1. **Ask for options, then choose.** *"Show me three versions"* turns the agent
   into a design studio — and gives you something concrete to show your community.
2. **Critique your own screens** with Moves 1–5 before asking for help. You'll
   catch most problems yourself.
3. **Always say the accessibility line.** Every build. It's the floor, not extra.

## The pre-ship ritual

Before you put it in front of your community, run through this. Frame every
question the same way: not *"is it correct?"* but **"will this delight the people I
made it for?"**

<Steps>

1. **The squint test** — blurred, does the right thing stand out on each screen?
2. **The breathing test** — calm and roomy, or cramped and busy?
3. **The consistency test** — do buttons, colours, and spacing match throughout?
4. **The stranger test** — could someone who's never seen it do the main task in silence?
5. **The welcome test** — can someone with low vision, a screen reader, shaky hands,
   or an old phone actually use it? *(Did I say the accessibility line?)*
6. **The belonging test** — would someone from my community feel it was made *by us,
   for us*?
7. **The honest test** — did I actually show this to real people and listen?

</Steps>

If you can say yes to these, you haven't just built something functional. You've
built something people will love — the only kind of pro-level work that matters.
